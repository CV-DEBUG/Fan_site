Biography

Ken Thompson is an American computer scientist best known for his pioneering work in the development of the Unix operating system. Born on February 4, 1943, in New Orleans, Louisiana, Thompson made significant contributions to computer science throughout his career. In the late 1960s and early 1970s, while working at Bell Labs, Thompson co-created Unix with Dennis Ritchie. Unix became a foundational operating system for many subsequent systems and has greatly influenced modern computing. Thompson also developed the B language, a precursor to the widely used C language, and is credited with co-authoring the original version of Unix in 1969. In addition to Unix, Thompson made major contributions to the development of plan 9 (a successor to Unix) and the Go programming language. He was awarded numerous honors for his work, including the Turing Award in 1983, one of the highest recognitions in computer science. Ken Thompson’s influence on the computing world is profound, particularly through Unix's design, which emphasized simplicity and efficiency—principles that remain integral to modern software systems.

News

Ken Thompson joined Google in 2006, where he played a key role in the development of the Go programming language. Designed as a successor to C, Go stands out for its simplicity, performance, and ability to handle concurrent programming efficiently, meeting the demands of modern infrastructures. However, Ken Thompson is no longer actively involved in the Go project or in full-time work at Google. He is now considered retired, though he remains an inspiring figure in the field of computer science.

Works and achievements

Ken Thompson is a key figure in modern computing. He notably created the B programming language in the 1970s and, more recently, the Go programming language. He is also the creator of the Unix operating system, which has had a profound influence on the development of modern operating systems. Thompson was awarded the Turing Award in 1983 and the National Medal of Technology in the United States in 1998.
